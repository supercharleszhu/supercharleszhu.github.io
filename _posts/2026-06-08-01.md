## Why this post

> 📚 **New to MoE?** If you're not sure what a Mixture-of-Experts model is or how the router, experts, and all-to-all are actually implemented, start with the companion post [Mixture-of-Experts Across the Stack]({% post_url 2026-06-05-01 %}) — it builds the mental model from a 20-line PyTorch reference up to wide expert parallelism. This post assumes that background and goes one level up, to the *serving-strategy* layer. This blog post will be more practical journey on optimizing MoE serving

Serving a Mixture-of-Experts model is not the same problem as serving a dense one. A dense model has exactly two ways to spread across GPUs — **tensor parallelism (TP)** and **data parallelism (DP)**. An MoE adds a third axis, **expert parallelism (EP)**. This post is a deep dive into those three strategies **as they apply to MoE serving** — what each shards, what collective it puts on the critical path, what it costs in memory, and when to pick it. It's grounded in three places: the **vLLM source** (traced down to the exact lines that pick the kernel), a **Qwen3-235B-A22B reward-judge benchmark** I ran on 8×H200, and three references that corroborate each other — AMD's [vLLM MoE parallelism guide](https://rocm.blogs.amd.com/software-tools-optimization/vllm-moe-guide/README.html), SGLang's [large-scale EP report](https://lmsys.org/blog/2025-05-05-large-scale-ep/), and the [vLLM data-parallel docs](https://docs.vllm.ai/en/latest/serving/data_parallel_deployment.html). The MoE mechanics themselves (router, grouped GEMM, the all-to-all kernel) get a full treatment in the [companion MoE post]({% post_url 2026-06-05-01 %}); here we're one level up, at the *serving-strategy* layer.

## The baseline: Serving Qwen3-235B-A22B with Tensor Parallelism (TP=8)

**TP shards everything and pays every layer.** Each rank holds `1/TP` of *every* weight matrix — attention q/k/v/o, the experts, the lm_head. After each sharded matmul it must `all-reduce` the partial activations. Lowest per-GPU memory, but a collective on the critical path of every layer.

For MoE specifically, **TP without EP means every expert is tensor-sharded across all ranks**. The router still picks the top-k experts for each token, but those chosen experts are not owned by one GPU. Each GPU holds a slice of every expert's weights, runs its slice of the expert GEMM, and then participates in the collective that merges the partial outputs. So TP saves memory by slicing the whole model evenly, but it also keeps the MoE layer coupled across every TP rank.

![Tensor parallelism for an MoE layer without expert parallelism](https://rocm.blogs.amd.com/_images/tp_moe_v2.svg)
_Tensor parallelism without expert parallelism: each expert is split across the TP group, so one routed expert still involves all ranks. Figure from AMD's [vLLM MoE parallelism guide](https://rocm.blogs.amd.com/software-tools-optimization/vllm-moe-guide/README.html)._

## 1. Three axes for spreading an MoE across GPUs

An MoE has two kinds of weights — the dense path (attention, norms, the router gate) and the experts — and the three parallelism axes act on them differently. The cleanest way to hold them in your head is **what each one shards vs. replicates**:

| | What it splits | What it replicates | Per-step communication | Works on |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **TP** | *every* layer, by hidden/head dim | nothing | **all-reduce** each layer | dense + MoE |
| **EP** | *only* MoE experts, by whole-expert | the rest (attention, etc., follow TP/DP) | dispatch + combine | **MoE only** (a flag, not standalone) |
| **DP** | nothing | the *whole model* (one copy per rank) | **none** — until you add EP | dense + MoE |

Three things fall out of that table that resolve most of the confusion:

**TP shards everything and pays every layer.** Each rank holds `1/TP` of *every* weight matrix — attention q/k/v/o, the experts, the lm_head. After each sharded matmul it must `all-reduce` the partial activations. Lowest per-GPU memory, but a collective on the critical path of every layer.

> 🔧 **Why it's *one* all-reduce per block, not one per matmul**
>
> "All-reduce after each sharded matmul" is the right intuition but counts slightly too many: an FFN/expert has *two* matmuls and an activation, yet pays only **one** all-reduce. The trick is the Megatron **column-parallel → element-wise → row-parallel** pairing:
>
> - **Up-proj (`W_gate`/`W_up`) is column-parallel** — sharded along the intermediate dim `I`. Rank *i* computes a **disjoint slice** of the intermediate, `[N, I/P]`. That's a *concatenation*, not a partial sum — each rank's neurons are already complete, so there is **nothing to reduce** after the first GEMM.
> - **SwiGLU is element-wise** along `I`: `silu(gate_i) ⊙ up_i` runs **entirely within rank i's slice** (gate and up are sharded identically), so the activation never needs another rank's data. The intermediate stays sharded straight through.
> - **Down-proj (`W_down`) is row-parallel** — now every rank produces a **partial sum** of the full output `[N, H]`. *That's* what the single `all-reduce` sums.
>
> So you pay a collective only when row-parallel turns the sharded intermediate back into a sum. If the middle op *mixed* across `I` (a norm or softmax over the intermediate dim), you'd be forced to all-gather/all-reduce *before* it — but element-wise activations never do, by design. Net: **attention = 1 all-reduce (after the O-proj), FFN/MoE = 1 all-reduce (after the down-proj).** (In the EP case the expert's two matmuls + SwiGLU are all *local* to one rank — zero intra-expert comm — and the one all-reduce is the cross-rank expert **combine** instead.)
{: .prompt-info }

**EP is not a third strategy — it's a modifier on the MoE half.** As AMD's guide puts it bluntly: *"Expert Parallelism is **not a standalone parallelism strategy**. It's a flag … that must be combined with TP or DP."* `--enable-expert-parallel` changes how the *expert* weights are split — from "shard every expert across ranks" (TP-style) to "give each rank a few *whole* experts" (EP-style) — and changes the MoE communication accordingly. Attention is still parallelized by whatever TP/DP you set.

**DP replicates and stays independent — unless EP couples it.** Plain DP is N copies of the model, each chewing its own requests, **zero inter-rank communication per layer**. The moment you add EP, that independence is gone (see §4).

## 2. The communication reality (the part everyone gets wrong)

Here is the fact that cost me an afternoon of confusion. In vLLM, the all-to-all only exists when there is **data parallelism**. From `fused_moe/config.py`:

```python
@property
def use_all2all_kernels(self):
    return self.dp_size > 1 and self.use_ep
```

Every dispatch/combine backend — DeepEP, `allgather_reducescatter`, the naive path — is gated on this flag. If it's `False`, vLLM's `maybe_make_prepare_finalize` factory **returns `None`**, no dispatch/combine kernel is built, and the MoE falls back to the standard grouped-GEMM whose partial outputs are merged with a plain **`all-reduce`**. So:

| Config | MoE communication |
|---|---|
| **TP only** (`dp=1`), EP off | all-reduce |
| **TP + EP** (`dp=1`) | **all-reduce** ← surprising, but it's EP for the *weights*, TP-style comm |
| DP, EP off | independent replicas (allgather/reducescatter inside a replica's MoE) |
| **DP + EP** (`dp>1`) | **all-to-all** (dispatch + combine) |

This is exactly what my trace showed (zero `ncclAllToAll`, all `ncclAllReduce`), and it's exactly what AMD's guide states independently: *"AllToAll kernels require `dp_size>1`. Since TP has `dp_size=1`, AllToAll is never triggered."*

So the word "EP" overloads two different things, and you have to say which you mean:

- **EP-the-weight-sharding** — each rank owns whole experts (memory win). You get this from `--enable-expert-parallel` *always*.
- **EP-the-all-to-all** — token dispatch/combine across ranks. You get this *only when `dp_size>1`*.

On a single tensor-parallel replica, "enabling EP" gives you the first and not the second. The reward judge below is precisely that case: experts genuinely partitioned (55 GiB/GPU, not 110), but combined with an all-reduce.

### The communication bytes, calculated

The table above says *what* collective each config runs; this says *how much* it moves. Take one layer's activation tensor as the unit: **S = N·H·2** bytes (bf16) — for the judge's prefill chunk (N=32768, H=4096) that's **S = 256 MiB**. A ring all-reduce moves **2(P−1)·S** across the group (P=8 → 14·S); an MoE all-to-all moves **2·fanout·S**, where **fanout = P·[1−(1−1/P)^k] ≈ 5.25** for top-k=8 on 8 ranks (each token reaches ~5.25 of 8 ranks, not all); a PP hand-off is one point-to-point send of S at each of the (p−1) stage boundaries. Over L=94 layers:

| config (8 GPUs) | per-layer comm | bytes / forward / GPU | aggregate / forward | dominant cost |
|---|---|---:|---:|---|
| **TP8 · EP8** (`dp=1`) | 2× all-reduce (attn + MoE) | **82 GiB** | 658 GiB | per-layer all-reduce |
| **TP8 · EP off** (`dp=1`, TP-MoE) | 2× all-reduce (attn + MoE) | **82 GiB** *(identical)* | 658 GiB | same comm; EP only moves compute/memory |
| **TP1 · EP8 · DP8** | 2× all-to-all (no attn all-reduce) | **31 GiB** | 247 GiB | all-to-all + §4 lockstep |
| **PP** (`p` stages) | (p−1)× p2p, boundaries only | **~0.25 GiB** (p=2) | (p−1)·S | the **bubble** (idle), not bytes |

(Per-layer in S units: all-reduce configs = 28·S/layer aggregate = 2 × 1.75·S per GPU; all-to-all = 2·fanout·S = 10.5·S/layer aggregate; PP = (p−1)·S for the *whole* forward.)

Three reads:

- **TP8·EP8 and TP8·EP-off move the *exact same* bytes.** Both all-reduce twice per layer over `[N,H]`. EP changes *which* matmul each rank runs (whole experts vs sliced) and the weight memory (55 vs 110 GiB/GPU) — **not the communication**. The ~4 s/step gap between them (§6) is a GEMM-shape effect, not a comm one.
- **TP1·EP8·DP8 moves ~2.6× *fewer* bytes** — but almost all of that is dropping the attention all-reduce (TP1), not the MoE primitive: the MoE all-to-all alone (10.5·S/layer) is only ~25% under the MoE all-reduce (14·S). And fewer bytes ≠ faster — NVLS all-reduce reduces *in the switch* (higher effective bandwidth than a token-permuting all-to-all), and the all-to-all is gated by the hottest rank (§4). On one node at k=8 it loses despite the lower byte count; it only pays once EP ≫ k (more ranks, sparser routing) over a slow inter-node fabric.
- **PP moves almost nothing** — a few hundred MiB of boundary p2p against tens of GiB of per-layer collectives. Its cost isn't bytes, it's the **bubble**; it earns its place offline / cross-node, where a deep request backlog hides the bubble and a small p2p beats a cross-node all-reduce.

### Case study: tracing EP8/TP8/dp=1 to the all-reduce in the vLLM 0.22 source

I hit this concretely serving a Qwen3-235B reward judge at **TP8 + EP8, dp=1** and wanted to find *where* the all-reduce comes from rather than the all-to-all I expected. vLLM 0.22 refactored the MoE forward into a `runner/` subpackage, so the path is `FusedMoE.forward → self.runner.forward → MoERunner` (`model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py`). Two `dp_size`-keyed gates decide the whole thing.

**Gate 1 — no all-to-all backend is even built** ([`fused_moe/config.py`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/config.py#L1034), pinned to `v0.22.0`):

```python
@property
def use_all2all_kernels(self):
    return self.dp_size > 1 and self.use_ep      # dp=1 → False
```

With it `False`, the dispatch object is never built. The factory [`maybe_make_prepare_finalize`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/all2all_utils.py#L105) hits its [`if not ...use_all2all_kernels`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/all2all_utils.py#L127) guard and returns `None` — the concrete dispatch/combine objects (`DeepEPHTPrepareAndFinalize`, `DeepEPLLPrepareAndFinalize`, `MoriPrepareAndFinalize`, the FlashInfer/`NixlEP` ones) are all constructed *below* that guard ([L148–L319](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/all2all_utils.py#L148)) and never reached. The `None` then makes [`maybe_init_modular_kernel`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L464) skip swapping in the modular all-to-all method, so the plain all-reduce path stands.

**Gate 2 — the token dispatch/combine is skipped** ([`runner/moe_runner.py`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py#L683)):

```python
@property
def do_naive_dispatch_combine(self) -> bool:
    return self.moe_config.dp_size > 1 and not self._quant_method.supports_internal_mk
```

So `_maybe_dispatch` / `_maybe_combine` only call `get_ep_group().dispatch_router_logits(...)` / `.combine(...)` when `dp_size > 1`. **At dp=1 the tokens are never shuffled across ranks** — each rank already holds the full TP-replicated token set.

**The reduce that *does* fire.** After the local experts run, the runner sums the partial per-rank outputs ([`runner/moe_runner.py`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py#L396), the "late all-reduce path" — the `tensor_model_parallel_all_reduce` is at [L416](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py#L416)):

```python
def _maybe_reduce_final_output(self, states, trunc_size):
    if (
        not self.moe_config.is_sequence_parallel
        and (self.moe_config.tp_size > 1 or self.moe_config.ep_size > 1)   # ep_size=8 → True
        and not self._fused_output_is_reduced
    ):
        states = tensor_model_parallel_all_reduce(states)                  # ← the all-reduce
    return states[..., :trunc_size]
```

The guard is `tp_size > 1 OR ep_size > 1`, and `ep_size = 8`, so it fires — a plain `tensor_model_parallel_all_reduce`.

**Putting it together:** EP genuinely partitions the experts (each rank owns 16 of 128 via the `expert_map`; weights drop to ~55 GiB/GPU), but with no dispatch the grouped GEMM runs *every* rank's local experts over the *whole* replicated batch (non-local experts skipped via `expert_id = -1`), and the partials are merged by the all-reduce above — never an all-to-all. The Kineto trace agreed: `fused_moe_kernel` + `ncclDevKernel_AllReduce` + `multimem_all_reduce`, and **zero `AllToAll`/`SendRecv`**. To move the token routing onto a real all-to-all you have to flip the one switch both gates read — `dp_size > 1` (i.e. DP attention + EP MoE).

> 🔧 **How the layer, runner, and kernel get wired up at init**
>
> The all-reduce-vs-all-to-all decision above is really a *kernel-selection* decision made once, at model build time. It's worth seeing the whole init pipeline, because vLLM 0.22 splits the MoE into three objects — a **layer** (`FusedMoE`), an execution **runner** (`MoERunner`), and a **kernel** (`FusedMoEKernel` = a *prepare/finalize* + an *experts GEMM*) — and "which kernel runs" is decided by which `dp_size`/quant combination you booted with.
>
> **Step 1 — the layer picks a quant method and allocates the (sharded) weights.** [`FusedMoE.__init__`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L105) first resolves a *quant method* in [`_get_quant_method`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L353): `self.quant_config.get_quant_method(...)` returns e.g. `Fp8MoEMethod` / `CompressedTensorsWNA16MoEMethod`, and if there's no quant config it falls back to [`UnquantizedFusedMoEMethod`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L362). That method's [`create_weights`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L419) allocates the expert weight tensors — and because EP shards by whole expert, each rank only allocates its `local_num_experts` (16 of 128 for our TP8+EP8 judge), which is *why* weights land at ~55 GiB/GPU instead of 110.
>
> **Step 2 — the layer constructs the runner.** [`self.runner = MoERunner(...)`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L427) hands the quant method, router, gate, and shared experts to [`MoERunner.__init__`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py#L218). The runner is the *execution* object — it wraps the shared experts in a `SharedExperts` helper and calls [`_select_forward`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py#L270), which binds the forward entry to the registered custom op `torch.ops.vllm.moe_forward` (or the `_shared` variant when shared experts exist). Note the runner does **not** itself choose a GEMM — it just holds `self._quant_method` and later calls through it.
>
> **Step 3 — the kernel is chosen *after* weights load, by pairing a dispatch impl with an experts GEMM.** This is the crux. [`maybe_init_modular_kernel`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L457) assembles a *modular kernel* from two halves:
>
> - the **prepare/finalize** (dispatch/combine) object from [`maybe_make_prepare_finalize`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/all2all_utils.py#L105) — `None` at `dp=1`, as traced above;
> - the **experts GEMM** from [`select_gemm_impl(prepare_finalize, layer)`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/fused_moe_method_base.py#L117), which each quant method overrides to return the right `FusedMoEExpertsModular` (Triton / DeepGEMM / CUTLASS / TRT-LLM / …) *based on the all2all impl and dtype*.
>
> [`FusedMoEModularMethod.make`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/fused_moe_modular_method.py#L50) glues them together — note the experts GEMM is literally a function *of* the dispatch object:
>
> ```python
> return FusedMoEModularMethod(
>     old_quant_method,
>     FusedMoEKernel(
>         prepare_finalize,                                       # dispatch / combine
>         old_quant_method.select_gemm_impl(prepare_finalize, moe_layer),  # experts GEMM, chosen from it
>         inplace=inplace,
>     ),
> )
> ```
>
> The layer then swaps `self.quant_method` to this modular method. **But at `dp=1` the first half is `None`**, so `maybe_init_modular_kernel` short-circuits (the `if prepare_finalize is not None` guard) and never builds a modular kernel — the quant method keeps its **monolithic** path (`is_monolithic = True`), i.e. a single fused-experts GEMM with no cross-rank dispatch, whose partial output is reduced by the runner's all-reduce. So "EP8/TP8/dp=1 → all-reduce" is exactly "no prepare/finalize was built, so the monolithic kernel was kept."
>
> **Step 4 — at runtime it's a thin call-through.** [`FusedMoE.forward`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/layer.py#L1306) calls [`runner.forward`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py#L594), which routes through [`_apply_quant_method`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/runner/moe_runner.py#L498): `is_monolithic ? apply_monolithic : apply`. The monolithic branch is the one our judge takes; its output then hits `_maybe_reduce_final_output` and the all-reduce fires. The modular branch (the `apply` side) is where DeepEP/CUTLASS dispatch-combine would have run, had `dp_size > 1` selected it at Step 3.
>
> So the whole "which collective" question is resolved at **build time, in Step 3**, by whether `maybe_make_prepare_finalize` returned a dispatch object — everything downstream is just executing the kernel that selection already locked in.

> 🧩 **Aside: Megatron-core folds the TP group into EP — vLLM only half does**
>
> A natural question once you see "TP8 + EP8, dp=1 → all-reduce": couldn't the engine instead *fold* the 8 tensor-parallel ranks into an 8-way expert-parallel group and run a proper all-to-all, the way **Megatron-core** does for training? The two frameworks handle this very differently, and the contrast pins down exactly what vLLM's `--enable-expert-parallel` does and doesn't give you.
>
> **Megatron-core decouples expert-TP from attention-TP.** Megatron exposes a separate knob, [`expert_tensor_parallel_size`](https://github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/blob/core_v0.17.1/megatron/core/parallel_state.py#L642) (ETP), defaulting to the attention `tensor_model_parallel_size`. The expert sub-network gets its **own** process-group layout, built from a dedicated [`expert_decoder_rank_generator`](https://github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/blob/core_v0.17.1/megatron/core/parallel_state.py#L802) with `tp=ETP, ep=EP, dp=expert_dp` (where `expert_dp = world / (ETP·EP·PP)`). Set `ETP < TP` and you *fold the attention-TP ranks into the expert dimension*: attention can run TP8 while the experts run **ETP1 × EP8** over the same 8 GPUs — each GPU owns whole experts, and the routed-token **all-to-all rides the EP group** ([`MoEAlltoAllTokenDispatcher`](https://github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/blob/core_v0.17.1/megatron/core/transformer/moe/token_dispatcher.py#L350): [`all_to_all(self.ep_group, ...)`](https://github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/blob/core_v0.17.1/megatron/core/transformer/moe/token_dispatcher.py#L671)), while any residual expert-TP work uses the combined [`tp-ep` group](https://github.com/NVIDIA/Megatron-LM/blob/core_v0.17.1/megatron/core/parallel_state.py#L1224). The fold is *flexible* — ETP can be anywhere from `TP` (pure expert-TP, no fold) down to `1` (full fold to EP) — and the all-to-all fires **regardless of data parallelism**.
>
> **vLLM has no such decoupling.** There is no `expert_tensor_parallel_size` in [`FusedMoEParallelConfig`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/config.py#L1206). `--enable-expert-parallel` is binary — and it *does* perform a degenerate **full** fold, setting the MoE's `tp_size = 1` and `ep_size = (model) tp_size`, with the source comment saying so outright:
>
> ```python
> # In EP, each device owns a set of experts fully. There is no tensor
> # parallel ...
> ep_size = tp_size
> ep_rank = tp_rank
> return FusedMoEParallelConfig(tp_size=1, ..., ep_size=ep_size, use_ep=True, ...)
> ```
>
> So vLLM *does* fold attention-TP ranks into EP at the **weight-layout** level (each rank owns whole experts). But two things differ from Megatron:
>
> 1. **No partial fold.** It's all-or-nothing: experts are either fully TP-sharded (`use_ep=False`) or fully EP-sharded (`use_ep=True`, `ep_size=TP`). There is no ETP2×EP4 middle ground.
> 2. **The fold does not buy you an all-to-all.** The dispatch all-to-all is gated *separately*, on [`use_all2all_kernels = dp_size > 1 and use_ep`](https://github.com/vllm-project/vllm/blob/v0.22.0/vllm/model_executor/layers/fused_moe/config.py#L1034). At `dp=1` the folded EP experts are still combined with a plain **all-reduce** — vLLM never builds the dispatch/combine kernel (exactly the trace above). Megatron's folded EP group all-to-alls regardless of DP; vLLM's only does when DP is on.
>
> So the intuition is right in the way that matters: **vLLM does not give you Megatron's decoupled TP→EP folding with an all-to-all over a dedicated EP group.** What vLLM calls "expert parallel" at `dp=1` is a weight-sharding fold *combined by all-reduce*; the dispatch all-to-all is reserved for the `dp_size>1` path. To get Megatron-style behavior (experts routed by all-to-all over a folded group, no data parallelism required), vLLM's only lever is to turn on DP attention — which is the lockstep regime of §4.
{: .prompt-info }

### What the per-rank trace shows: equal experts, unequal work, equal wall-clock

The all-reduce combine has a second-order consequence that only shows up when you profile *every* rank, not one. EP gives each rank **whole** experts (16 of 128), so the weights are evenly split — but the router sends an **uneven** number of tokens to each rank's experts, so the per-rank *compute* is skewed. The all-reduce barrier then flattens that skew back into equal wall-clock. I captured a 40 s Kineto window across all 8 ranks of the TP8+EP8 (`dp=1`) judge (sorted by compute):

| rank | compute (ms) | MoE+GEMM (ms) | all-reduce (ms) | compute+comm |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0 (heaviest) | **19794** | 16622 | **11639** | 31433 |
| 4 | 19115 | 15960 | 12324 | 31439 |
| 2 | 18670 | 15512 | 12794 | 31463 |
| 1 | 18616 | 15471 | 12876 | 31492 |
| 5 | 18183 | 15067 | 13118 | 31301 |
| 3 | 17495 | 14378 | 13972 | 31468 |
| 7 | 17461 | 14358 | 13986 | 31447 |
| 6 (lightest) | **17384** | 14267 | **14095** | 31479 |

Three things fall out, all consistent with "all-reduce, no all-to-all":

1. **Compute skews ~15%** — `max/min` = **1.14** overall, **1.17** on the MoE GEMMs alone. Equal expert *weights*, unequal token *load* → unequal FLOPs (the grouped GEMM's `M` dimension is "tokens routed to this rank's experts," which the router decides per step). This is the EP expert-load imbalance, mild here only because Qwen3's router is aux-loss-balanced — no EPLB, no capacity dropping.
2. **The all-reduce is the exact inverse.** The heaviest-compute rank (0) waits the *least* in the collective (11.6 s); the lightest (6) waits the *most* (14.1 s) — `corr ≈ −1`. The all-reduce is a barrier, so light ranks finish their GEMMs early and idle **inside** the collective, waiting for rank 0.
3. **Total (compute + comm) is flat — ~31.4 s on every rank, ±0.6%.** Lockstep: the skew lives entirely in the compute/wait *split*, never the wall-clock; all 8 ranks exit the step together, gated by the heaviest.

So the `dp=1` TP+EP path has its own quiet version of §4's lockstep — not from a DP coordinator, but from the per-layer all-reduce absorbing the routing skew into idle barrier time. And the top kernels pin the primitive one last time: `ncclDevKernel_AllReduce_Sum_bf16` (the single dominant line) and `multimem_all_reduce`, with `cutlass …MoeFCGemm` doing the skewed expert work — and **zero** all-to-all / dispatch / SendRecv kernels anywhere in the trace.

## 3. Memory: who pays what

The three strategies have opposite memory profiles, and this often decides the choice before throughput does.

**Weights.** TP shards *everything*, so it's the most memory-efficient per GPU. DP replicates the full model (worst). **DP+EP is in between, and asymmetric: experts are EP-sharded, but attention is *replicated* across DP ranks** — so per-GPU weights are *higher* than TP at the same GPU count. Measured on Qwen3-235B (8×H200, the ~8.5 B non-expert params are what get replicated):

| Layout | weights/GPU | KV/GPU | peak/GPU |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| **TP8 + EP8** (`dp=1`) | **55.0 GiB** | 46.1 GiB (one pool) | **~127 GiB** |
| **DP2 / TP4 + EP8** | 56.7 GiB | 24.7 GiB ×2 pools | **~140 GiB** (near-OOM) |

**KV cache.** TP keeps one shared pool sharded across ranks. **DP gives each rank its own independent KV pool** — which is the whole point of "DP attention": more aggregate KV ⇒ bigger batch. AMD's numbers make it vivid: a DP+EP layout that leaves *"0.125 GB KV per GPU"* against TP+EP's *"1 GB"* enables *"8× larger effective batch sizes."* (Note `--max-num-seqs` is **per DP rank**, so concurrency scales with DP size.)

The catch visible above: pushing DP also pushed *peak* memory to ~140 GiB on a ~143 GiB card — because TP4 shards attention activations less than TP8, and the DP all-to-all needs staging buffers. For a server that has to **share the node** (our reward judge colocates with the rollout + actor — see [how that's wired]({% post_url 2026-06-05-01 %})), that near-OOM peak is disqualifying on its own.

## 4. The hidden cost of DP+EP: lockstep

Plain DP ranks are embarrassingly parallel. **Add EP and they become a single lockstepped machine**, because the expert all-to-all is a collective — every rank must reach it together, every MoE layer.

Three concrete taxes, all of which I saw or the docs name:

1. **A per-step barrier.** Routing is dynamic, so before each all-to-all the ranks must agree on shapes. vLLM logs it: `Using CPU all reduce to synchronize DP padding between ranks` — a tiny CPU all-reduce *every forward* to take the max token count and pad to it.
2. **Dummy forwards for idle ranks.** The vLLM docs: *"expert layers across all ranks are required to synchronize during every forward pass, even when there are fewer requests than DP ranks"* — so an idle rank still runs an empty forward (managed by a *"separate DP Coordinator process"*) just to keep the collective alive.
3. **Straggler tail.** Everyone waits for the slowest/longest-prompt rank each step. In my DP2/TP4 run, p95 latency went **7.8 s → 10 s** versus TP8 even though medians were close — the classic lockstep signature.

This is why "wide EP" is a *systems* project, not a flag. The state of the art exists to claw the lockstep back:

- **Two-Batch Overlap (TBO)** — split the batch so one micro-batch's expert compute hides the other's all-to-all. SGLang reports **27–35%** prefill throughput from it — *but* **−27% at 32 tokens/device**, i.e. it hurts at small batch.
- **EPLB (expert-parallel load balancing)** — replicate hot experts as redundant copies. SGLang: **1.49× prefill / 2.54× decode**.
- **A real all-to-all kernel** — [DeepEP](https://github.com/deepseek-ai/DeepEP), which moves only the routed top-k tokens over NVSHMEM (see the [NVSHMEM deep dive]({% post_url 2026-06-08-00 %}) for the one-sided transport it rides on). **Without** DeepEP, vLLM's default `allgather_reducescatter` *gathers every rank's tokens to every rank* — which in my benchmark made the MoE GEMM time *go up* (595 → 905 ms) and total comm *go up* (562 → ~893 ms) versus the TP8 all-reduce. The fallback is a regression; the win needs the real kernel.

## 5. When to choose what

Putting it together — a decision framework, with the crossover numbers from AMD's benchmarks:

### By concurrency (the first cut)
- **Latency / low concurrency (≤128 in-flight):** **TP**. One replica, every GPU on every token, lowest TTFT. AMD measured *"40–86% higher throughput"* and *"80% lower TTFT"* for TP vs DP here; for DeepSeek-R1, TP8+EP was *"52% higher throughput"* than DP8+EP at low load.
- **Throughput / high concurrency (≥512 in-flight):** **DP** (replicas). AMD: *"16–47% higher throughput at scale"*; DeepSeek-R1 DP8+EP hit *"7,114 tok/s (47% higher than TP8+EP)."*
- **Crossover: 256–512 concurrent requests** across the models they tested. Below it TP wins, above it DP wins.

### By the MoE's footprint and load (the second cut)
*(A dense model has no experts and thus no EP — it's just TP for latency / a model too big for one GPU, DP replicas for throughput. Everything below is MoE-specific, which is where the real choices live.)*
- **MoE that fits in ≤8 GPUs, single replica, latency- or memory-bound:** **TP + EP** (`dp=1`). Experts sharded for the memory win, combined with an all-reduce — vLLM's weight-only fold (§2 aside). Simple, no lockstep, no DeepEP. *(This is the reward judge in §6.)*
- **MoE at scale / decode-heavy / throughput-bound:** **DP attention + EP all-to-all**, with DeepEP + EPLB + TBO. This is the DeepSeek/SGLang wide-EP regime — and the only vLLM path that actually builds the dispatch/combine kernel. SGLang ran **EP32 for prefill, EP72 for decode** (disaggregated), hitting **$0.20 / 1M output tokens** and *"up to 5×"* over a TP16 baseline.

### Two routing rules that override the above
- **Expert activation density.** Ultra-sparse models can lose to EP because the all-to-all dominates: AMD found **Llama-4-Maverick (0.78% density) runs 7–12% *faster* with EP off**. Their rule of thumb: **< 1% density → EP off; > 3% → EP on.**
- **MLA / single-KV-head models (DeepSeek):** **DP attention is essentially required** — a single latent KV head can't be sharded usefully by TP, so you replicate attention (DP) and EP the experts.

## 6. Worked example: the 235B reward judge

The constraints made the choice, and they're a good template for "single-model serving" rather than "frontier deployment":

- **One node, 8 GPUs**, and the judge must **colocate** with the RL rollout + actor (so it can't hog memory or all 8 GPUs to itself).
- **Single replica.** No fleet, no PD disaggregation.
- **Prefill-bound** workload: ~2000-token prompts, 4-token outputs.

**Chosen: TP8 + EP8 (`dp=1`).** EP shards the 128 experts → **55 GiB/GPU** weights (vs 110 at TP4), which un-starves the KV cache; the combine is a plain all-reduce; **55.6 s** to drain one step's 2048 calls, peak ~127 GiB (room to colocate). Prefill-bound, KV only ~9% used — exactly the "shard experts for memory, all-reduce combine" sweet spot from §5.

**Rejected: DP2/TP4 + EP8.** I benchmarked it to be sure. It was **slower (59–62 s), near-OOM (140 GiB, can't colocate), and worse-tailed (p95 10 s)** — because with no DeepEP the `allgather_reducescatter` fallback gathered all tokens to all ranks (MoE GEMM 595→905 ms, comm 562→893 ms) and added the per-step lockstep. The profiler told the whole story: for this workload DP+EP *added* compute and comm. It's a throughput/wide-deployment play, and this is neither.

The lesson generalizes: **DP+EP is for many requests across many GPUs with a real all-to-all kernel and a load balancer.** For a single-replica, memory-constrained, latency-ish MoE server, **TP+EP is the right tool** — and you should expect (and will see) an all-reduce, not an all-to-all.

## Decision tree (the compressed version)

```
Serving an MoE model:
  activation density < 1% (ultra-sparse)?  → EP off; fall back to TP or DP replicas
  single replica, ≤8 GPUs, latency/memory-bound?
     → TP + EP (dp=1)   [experts sharded, ALL-REDUCE combine, no lockstep, no DeepEP]
  high concurrency / decode-heavy / wide deployment?
     → DP attention + EP all-to-all (dp>1)   [the only path that builds dispatch/combine]
         requires: DeepEP (NVSHMEM) + EPLB + TBO, else the fallback regresses
  MLA / single-KV-head (DeepSeek)?  → DP attention is required
  Concurrency crossover (all of the above): TP below ~256–512 in-flight, DP above.

(Dense model? No experts, no EP — just TP for latency / too-big-for-1-GPU, or DP replicas for throughput.)
```

## The one-paragraph summary

Serving an MoE comes down to three knobs. **TP** shards every layer and all-reduces every layer (memory-cheap, latency-friendly, one replica). **DP** replicates the model and stays independent (throughput, more memory) — until you add **EP**, which couples the ranks into a per-step all-to-all lockstep. EP is not a third strategy: it's a modifier that shards the *experts*, and in vLLM it only produces an actual all-to-all when `dp_size>1` — so "enabling EP" on a single TP group gives you partitioned experts combined with an **all-reduce** (a weight-only fold; Megatron's decoupled ETP/EP would route them through an all-to-all, vLLM's won't), not the dispatch/combine you pictured. Pick TP+EP for a single-replica, memory- or latency-bound MoE; pick DP-attention+EP (with DeepEP, EPLB, and TBO) only when you're serving enough concurrency to amortize the all-to-all — roughly past the 256–512-request crossover.
